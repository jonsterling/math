1. Geometric universes [0023]

[0022] Definition 1·a (Geometric universe).

A geometric universe $E$ is defined to be a finitely complete cartesian closed category with a subobject classifier and a natural numbers object.

[0036] Definition 1·b (Morphisms of geometric universes).

We describe three different kinds of morphism between geometric universes.

  1. A partial cartesian morphism $f : E \to F$ is a pullback preserving functor $f:E\to F$.
  2. A cartesian morphism $f : E \to F$ is a partial cartesian morphism $f : E\to F$ that preserves the terminal object.
  3. A logical morphism $f : E \to F$ is a cartesian morphism $f : E \to F$ that additionally preserves the subobject classifier.
  4. An algebraic morphism $f : E \to F$ is a pair $f = (\InvImg{f}\dashv \DirImg{f})$ such that $\InvImg{f} : E \to F$ a cartesian morphism of geometric universes. The left adjoint $\InvImg{f}$ is called the inverse image and the right adjoint $\DirImg{f}$ is called the direct image.
[0038] Remark 1·c (Finite colimits, natural numbers objects, and logical functors).

Note that any geometric universe has finite colimits, which can be constructed using the subobject classifier. A logical morphism between geometric universes necessarily preserves finite colimits for this reasons. Using Freyd’s characterization of natural numbers objects in terms of a coequalization property, it follows that logical morphisms also preserve the natural numbers object.

[0037] Definition 1·d (2-categories of geometric universes).

Each of the different notions of geometric universe above gives rise to a 2-category. We will fix our conventions below:

  1. $\PCartGU$ is the 2-category of geometric universes, partial cartesian morphisms, and natural transformations between them.
  2. $\CartGU$ is the 2-category of geometric universes, cartesian morphisms, and natural transformations between them.
  3. $\LogGU$ is the locally groupoidal 2-category of geometric universes, logical morphisms, and natural isomorphisms between them.
  4. $\AlgGU$ is the 2-category of geometric universes and algebraic morphisms $f = (\InvImg{f}\dashv\DirImg{f})$, such that a 2-morphism $f \to g$ is given by a natural transformation $\InvImg{f}\to\InvImg{g}$.

1.1. Fibrations of geometric universes [0039]

Logical morphisms play an important role in the study of geometric universes; in particular, the most important notion of fibered geometric universe involves logical morphisms rather than algebraic morphisms.

[003A] Definition 1.1·a (Logical fibrations of geometric universes).

Let $B$ be a geometric universes; a logical fibration of geometric universes over $B$ is given by a fibered category $E$ over $B$ such that each that fiber category $E_u$ is a geometric universe and each restriction map $E_v\to E_u$ is a logical morphism.

[003C] Theorem 1.1·b (Moens' Theorem).

Let $E$ be a logical fibration of geometric universes over a geometric universe $B$. Then the following are equivalent:

  1. $E$ has stable and disjoint sums over $B$.
  2. $E$ is the gluing fibration for a cartesian morphism $B\to F$ of geometric universes.

Indeed, in the situation described above we may define $F$ to be the fiber $E\Sub{\ObjTerm{B}}$ over the terminal object. The functor $B\to E\Sub{\ObjTerm{B}}$ sends each $b\in B$ to the sum $\coprod_{b}\ObjTerm{E}$.

[003D] Theorem 1.1·c (Jibladze's Theorem).

Let $E$ be a logical fibration of geometric universes over $B$; if $E$ has small sums over $B$, then these sums are stable and disjoint.

[003E] Corollary 1.1·d.

Combining Theorem 1.1·b [003C] and Theorem 1.1·c [003D], the following are equivalent for a logical fibration $E$ of geometric universes over $B$:

  1. $E$ has small sums over $B$.
  2. $E$ is the gluing fibration for a cartesian morphism $B \to F$ of geometric universes.

It will follow in the scenario of Corollary 1.1·d [003E], the total category $\TotCat{E}$ is a geometric universe and the projection $\TotCat{E}\to B$ is both a logical morphism of geometric universes and an algebraic morphism of geometric universes. Moreover, the Artin gluing theory of geometric universes will reveal that there exists a subterminal object $U\rightarrowtail \ObjTerm{\TotCat{E}}$ such that $\TotCat{E}\to B$ factors as $\TotCat{E}\to \Sl{\TotCat{E}}{U} \simeq B$. Then $F = E\Sub{\ObjTerm{B}}$ will be revealed to be equivalent to the full subcategory of $\TotCat{E}$ spanned by objects $X$ such that the projection $X\times U\to U$ is an isomorphism.
