[0022] Definition 1·a (Geometric universe).

A geometric universe $E$ is defined to be a cartesian closed category equipped with a subobject classifier. A morphism $f : E \to F$ of geometric universes is given by a functor $\InvImg{f}:F\to E$ preserving finite limits equipped with a right adjoint $\DirImg{f}: E\to F$. A 2-morphism $f\to g$ is given by a natural transformation $\InvImg{f}\to \InvImg{g}$, or equivalently, a natural transformation $\DirImg{g}\to\DirImg{f}$.

The left adjoint $\InvImg{f} : F \to E$ is called the inverse image and the right adjoint $\DirImg{f} : E \to F$ is called the direct image.

1.1. Relative and fibered geometric universes [0027]

Just as it is important to study relative categories in addition to absolute categories, the same is true of geometric universes. The main way for a geometric universe $F$ to vary in a geometric universe $E$ is for it to be equipped with a structure map $f : E \to F$. This should at first be slightly counter-intuitive, because usually we think of something varying over rather than under a base. The reason for the present orientation will become more clear when we study below the relative geometric universe $f : E \to F$ in terms of a fibered category in the sense of [0002].

[0028] Definition 1.1·a (The gluing fibration).

Let $f : E \to F$ be a morphism of geometric universes; the properties of such a morphism are profitably accessed through its gluing fibration $\GL{f}$ over $E$, obtained as the base change [0007] along the inverse image $\InvImg{f} : E \to F$ of the canonical self-indexing [0003] of $F$.

The gluing fibration $\GL{f}$ provides a solid intuition for the way in which $F$ varies in $E$: we are concerned with the variation of families of $F$-objects indexed in $E$ objects.
